the words of the Lord, where He says, "Why asketh thou me about good?
there is none good, but One, that is, God;" provided only that the
substance of the Father be not therefore believed to be of greater
goodness than that of the Son, according to which He is the Word by
whom all things were made; and if there is nothing in it abhorrent from
sound doctrine; let us securely use it, and not one explanation only, but
as many as we are able to find. For so much the more powerfully are the
heretics proved wrong, the more outlets are open for avoiding their
snares. But let us now start afresh, and address ourselves to the
consideration of that which still remains.
